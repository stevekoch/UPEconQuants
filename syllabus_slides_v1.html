Course Description

  • Quants generally covers some mathematical economics, some probability and some fairly standard regression ideas.

  • By this time, it is expected that you have a background at least to the level of Goodwin’s text, which you can find at https://rtgodwin.com/quantecon.pdf

  • Rather than a focus on standard regression ideas, this module will focus more on useful applications, as well as some deeper testing and nonlinearities. We will not realistically have much time for machine learning…

Course Objectives

  • There are plenty of topics to consider - but I have limited the focus to data manipulation and presentation, some important distributional ideas, and, finally, conditional outcomes. Along the way, we will certainly see math and probability.

  • I would like students to understand the importance of repeatability, and using computers (and readme files, at the very least) to improve data presentation and analysis.

  • I would like students to understand the difference in data types and approaches to presenting and analyzing those different types.

  • I would like students to be able to use a variety of approaches, when they undertake their analysis.

Software I

  • Goodwin’s text also includes some R, which you might find helpful.

  • I work in R, because I care about repeatability, which R and associated options make easy; many of these options are also availalble for Stan and Python (even Stata, although not quite as seemlessly).

  • Numerous other statistical packages can do what we will do in the next few months, and AI tools can help you program things.

Software II

  • I will not devote my time to teaching everything you might want to know about R or any other language.

  • I use R, because there are no costs involved in its purchase. It is also widely appreciated by the Machine Learning community, many even suggesting it is faster and more useful than python (but opinions vary on this).

  • R is exceptionally flexible, can be incorporated directly into documents (such as reports and research manuscripts) and there is an army of online support for code help.

Grading I

  • There are four ‘projects’ through the term.

  • Each will require some sort of clear ‘readme’ file that outlines clearly the exact steps to follow, the decisions made to undertake the analysis, and so on.

  • Furthermore, each will be underpinned by a report that properly references software, data, software and relevant packages (if needed).

  • Of course, the report should describe the project, some relevant statistics or modelling ‘theory’, present and describe the results, inclusive of cross-references and notes for tables and figures.

  • I do hope to keep the writing component down to 4-5 pages.

Grading II

  • The projects will build on each other, beginning with accessing data - students will generally be asked to make use of different data sources.

  • Students will then need to present the data through tables and descriptive illustrations. From there, the data will be used for different types of analysis related to the various topics discussed in the module.

  • Each of the projects is 25% of the final mark.

Grading III

  • Grading occurs across the following components:

    • (1) Ease of replication, which is underscored by the quality of the comments and related documentation

    • (2) Correctness of the completion of the activities and

    • (3) Clarity and reasonableness of the discussion contained within the ‘report’ document.

  • Assignments will have a submission deadline. I presume they will be emailed, although I might include upload via click-up. Late projects are reduced 10% for each day late (up to 6 days). No project will be accepted a week late or more.
