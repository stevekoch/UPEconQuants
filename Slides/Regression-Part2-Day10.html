Variances and Testing

As we showed last time

  • The variance definition

\[ V(\hat{\beta}|X) = E \left[ \left( \hat{\beta} - \beta \right) \left( \hat{\beta} - \beta \right)' \bigg| X \right] \]

  • In matrix notation

\[ V(\hat{\beta}| X) = E \Biggl[ [X'X]^{-1}X'uu'X[X'X]^{-1} \Bigg|X \Biggr] \]

How to estimate it?

  • Basic analogy principle
  • Use an ‘estimate’ for an actual value
  • Thus:

\[ V(\hat{\beta}| X) = E \Biggl[ [X'X]^{-1}X'\hat{u}\hat{u}'X[X'X]^{-1} \Bigg|X \Biggr] \]

Some thoughts

  • We do not expect any particular \(\hat{u}_i\) to be zero
  • Instead, \(E[\hat{u}|X] = 0\)
  • Technically, we are squaring, anyway..
  • Take a look at the sandwich package

Some ‘corrections’

  1. HC0: use \(\hat{u}_i^2\)

  2. HC1: use \(\frac{n}{n-k} \cdot \hat{u}_i^2\)

  3. HC2: use \(\frac{\hat{u}_i^2}{1-h_{ii}}\), where \(h_{ii}\) is the term from the predictor matrix discussed last week

  4. HC3: use \(\frac{\hat{u}_i^2}{(1-h_{ii})^2}\)

  5. HC4: use \(\frac{\hat{u}_i^2}{(1-h_{ii})^{\delta_i}}\), where \(\delta_i = \min \{4, h_i/\overline{h} \}\)

  6. HC1 - HC4 are small sample adjustments

Clusters

  1. Really only for HC2 and HC3

  2. HC2: use \(\frac{G-1}{G} \left(I_{nG} - H_{GG} \right)^{-1}\), where \(nG\) is the number of observations in cluster \(G\), and \(H_{GG}\) is the prediction matrix for cluster \(G\).

  3. HC3: use \(\frac{G-1}{G} \left(I_{nG} - H_{GG} \right)^{-2}\), where \(nG\) is the number of observations in cluster \(G\), and \(H_{GG}\) is the prediction matrix for cluster \(G\).

  4. There are further corrections for multiple cluster dimensions, which we might see in panels, for instance.

  5. Bootstrapping is also an option..

Testing: Single Parameter

Back to the variance: a quadratic form

\[ \begin{split} \left( \hat{\beta}-\beta \right) \left( \hat{\beta}-\beta \right)' &= \left( \left(X'X\right)^{-1}X'u \right) \left( \left(X'X\right)^{-1}X'u \right)' \\ &= \left(X'X\right)^{-1}X'uu'X\left(X'X\right)^{-1} \\ &= A^{-1} B A^{-1} \end{split} \]

  • So, let us look at \(A\) and \(B\)

Pieces of the QF: \(A\)

  • Start with \(A\), which is from observed data

\[ \begin{split} A &= X'X \\ \hat{A} &= N^{-1} \left( \sum_{i=1}^N x_i'x_i \right) \end{split} \]

  • Simple average
  • Built-up from the observation level

Pieces of the QF: \(B\)

  • Let us look at \(B\) (has unobserved information)

\[\begin{equation}\begin{split} B &= X'uu'X \\ \hat{B} &= N^{-1} \left( \sum_{i=1}^N x_i'\hat{u}_i^2 x_i \right) \\ &= N^{-1} \left( \sum_{i=1}^N \hat{u}_i^2x_i'x_i \right) \end{split}\end{equation}\]

Distribution of estimators I

  • Substituting sample means for population means

\[ \begin{aligned} \hat{\beta} &\xrightarrow{p} \left( \frac{X'X}{N} \right)^{-1} \left( \frac{X'y}{N} \right) = \beta \\ \widehat{V \left[ \hat{\beta} \right] } & \xrightarrow{p} \left( \frac{X'X}{N} \right)^{-1} \left( \frac{ X'uu'X}{N} \right) \left(\frac{X'X}{N} \right)^{-1} \\ &= N^{-1} A^{-1}BA^{-1} \end{aligned} \]

Distribution of estimators II

  • Taking advantage of one of the many CLTs

\[ \begin{split} \hat{\beta} &\xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N} \left( \beta , N^{-1}\hat{A}^{-1}\hat{B}\hat{A}^{-1} \right) \\ \hat{\beta} - \beta &\xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N} \left( 0 , N^{-1}\hat{A}^{-1}\hat{B}\hat{A}^{-1} \right) \\ \sqrt{N} \left(\hat{\beta} - \beta \right) &\xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N} \left( 0 , \hat{A}^{-1}\hat{B}\hat{A}^{-1} \right) \\ \end{split} \]

Heteroscedasticity-robust test statistics

  • Pull out \(t-\) statistics or \(z-\)statistics (using \(z\) for simplicity)
  • Replace \(A^{-1}BA^{-1}\) with \(\hat\Omega\)

\[ \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{ \sqrt{\hat\Omega[j,j] /N} } \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N} (0,1) \]

Testing: Multiple Parameter

An example setup

  • Cobb-Douglas production model

\[ y=AK^{\beta_0} L^{\beta_1} E^{\beta_2} \]

  • OLS in log

\[ \ln y= \ln A + \beta_0 \ln K + \beta_1 \ln L + \beta_2 \ln E + u \]

A common assumption

  • Constant returns to scale?

\[ \beta_0 + \beta_1 + \beta_2 = 1 \]

  • A linear restriction
  • On multiple parameters

Imposing restrictions under homoscedasticity

  • Homoscedasticity leads to an \(F-\)test
  • General restriction is \(R\beta=r\)

\[ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \beta_0 \\ \beta_1 \\ \beta_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \end{pmatrix} \]

Apply variance rules

  • Under Homoscedasticity

\[ \tilde{\beta} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N} \left( \beta, \sigma^2 \left( X'X \right)^{-1} \right) \]

  • The distribution of \(R\tilde\beta\)

\[ R\tilde{\beta} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N} \left( R \beta, \sigma^2 R \left( X'X \right)^{-1} R'\right) \]

Adjusting the variances

  • Squared standard normal is distributed as a \(\chi^2_{J}\)
  • \(J\) is number of restrictions or “\(=\)” signs…

\[ Q_1 = \frac{ \left( R\tilde{\beta} - R\beta \right)' \left[R \left( X'X \right)^{-1} R' \right]^{-1} \left( R\tilde{\beta} - R\beta \right)}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_J \]

  • By similar reasoning,

\[ Q_2 = \frac{ (N-k) \hat{\sigma}^2 } {\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{N-k} \]

Developing the \(F\)

  • Ratio of two \(\chi^2\) random variables, normalized by their degrees of freedom
  • Yields an \(F-\)statistic (\(\sigma^2\) terms cancel, so not included)

\[ \lambda = \frac{ \left( R\tilde{\beta} - R\beta \right)' \left[R \left( X'X \right)^{-1} R' \right]^{-1} \left( R\tilde{\beta} - R\beta \right)}{ \hat{\sigma}^2} \cdot \frac{N-k}{J} \sim F_{J,N-k} \]

Impose the restriction

\[ \lambda = \frac{ \left( R\tilde{\beta} - r \right)' \left[R \left( X'X \right)^{-1} R' \right]^{-1} \left( R\tilde{\beta} - r \right)}{ \hat{\sigma}^2} \cdot \frac{N-k}{J} \sim F_{J,N-k} \]

  • After some manipulation

\[ \left( R\tilde{\beta} - r \right)' \left[R \left( X'X \right)^{-1} R' \right]^{-1} \left( R\tilde{\beta} - r \right) = \text{ESS}_U - \text{ESS}_R \]

The test statistic you might remember?

  • The statistic

\[ \frac{ \left(R^2_U - R^2_R \right) /J} {\left( 1-R^2_U \right)/(N-k)} \sim F_{J,N-k} \]

  • What does it ‘say’??

Heteroscedasticity effects

  • Heteroskedasticity invalidates the standard \(F\)
    • A heteroskedasticity robust version exists
    • Here, I describe the Wald version (instead)
  • Define a general linear hypothesis \(R\beta=r\)
  • This is as before

Return to the distribution of the estimator

\[ \begin{split} \hat{\beta} &\xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N} \left( \beta , N\Omega \right) \\ R \hat{\beta} &\xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N} \left( R \beta , NR\Omega R' \right) \\ R \hat{\beta} -r &\xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N} \left( R \beta -r , NR\Omega R' \right) \\ R \hat{\beta} -r &\xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N} \left( 0 , NR\Omega R' \right) \\ \end{split} \]

  • From this distribution, we can develop a \(\chi^2\) distribution

Needed pieces

  • Definitions:
    • Standard normal deviate: A random sample from standard normal distribution
    • \(\chi^2\) Distribution: Sum of squared normal deviates
    • \(\chi^2\) Degrees of Freedom: The number of standard normal deviates being summed
  • Above deviate is not standard normal
  • How do we do this in one dimension (Recall from early stats)?

Extending

  • How does that translate to multiple dimensions?
  • Taking from above
    • \(R\hat{\beta} - r\) is zero mean normal variate
    • We need to divide by square root of variance
    • Requires matrix decomposition, let us skip

With this in mind…

\[ k^{-1}(R \hat{\beta} -r)' (R\Omega R')^{-1} (R \hat{\beta} -r) \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_k \]

  • ‘Divided’ the squared normal deviate by its variance \((R\Omega R')^{-1}\)
  • Equivalent to squaring a standard normal deviate
  • This is a Wald test, rather than an \(F\)

Test Estimate

  • We use the same model from before, but test all the parameters on settlement.
  • The test statistics will be for homoskedasticity, thus an F, as well as both heteroskedastic and cluster-heteroskedastic models

Decomposition

Counterfactual type questions

  • How might female outcomes (wages, e.g.) change if they were men?
  • We cannot make them men, but we can give them the data characteristics of men
  • Decomposition is one way to examine this

Atheoretical construct

  • Define a binary “treatment”
  • Assume it is exogenous

\[ D_i = \begin{cases} 1, &\text{If male} \\ 0, & \text{If female} \end{cases} \]

  • Define “treatment” potential outcomes:

\[ Y_i = \begin{cases} Y_{1i}, &\text{Outcome, if male} \\ Y_{0i}, & \text{Outcome, if female} \end{cases} \]

Some formality

  • Condition \(Y\) on other variables
  • Separate regressions for both groups

\[ \begin{aligned} Y_{0i} &= x_{1i} \beta_0 + u_{0i} \\ Y_{1i} &= x_{0i} \beta_1 + u_{1i} \end{aligned} \]

  • Responses might differ
  • Characteristics might differ
  • Ignored for now: Structure could differ

Decomposition foundation

  • Define our estimate of interest

\[ \hat{\Delta} = \overline{Y}_{1i} - \overline{Y}_{0i} \]

  • Rewrite a previous useful assumption
  • \(E[D_i u_{0i}] = E[D_i u_{1i}] = 0\)
  • Why this assumption?
  • Eliminates unobservable effects

Thus…

\[ \hat{\Delta} = \overline{x}_0 \left( \hat{\beta}_1 - \hat{\beta}_0 \right) + \hat{\beta}_1 \left( \overline{x}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \right) \]

Derivation…

\[ \begin{split} \hat\Delta_2 =& \overline{x}_1 \hat{\beta}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_0 + \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_1 \\ =& \overline{x}_0\left( \hat{\beta}_1 - \hat{\beta}_0 \right) + \hat{\beta}_1 \left( \overline{x}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \right) \end{split} \]

  • “2” for twofold decomposition
  • It includes a \(\beta\) component: unexplained
  • It includes an \(x\) component: explained
  • What is meant by explained and unexplained?

Extending Decomposition

Basic setup, repeated

  • Recall the basic model

\[ \begin{aligned} Y_{0i} &= x_{0i} \beta_0 + u_{0i} \\ Y_{1i} &= x_{1i} \beta_1 + u_{1i} \end{aligned} \]

  • We want to look at

\[ \hat{\Delta} = \overline{Y}_1 - \overline{Y}_0 = \overline{x}_1 \hat{\beta}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_0 \]

Including an interaction term

  • Derivation

\[ \begin{split} \Delta_3 =& \overline{x}_1 \hat{\beta}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_0 + \left( \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_0 - \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_0 \right) + \left( \overline{x}_1 \hat{\beta}_0 - \overline{x}_1 \hat{\beta}_0 \right) + \left( \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_1 \right) \\ =& \overline{x}_0\left( \hat{\beta}_1 - \hat{\beta}_0 \right) + \hat{\beta}_0 \left( \overline{x}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \right) + \hat{\beta}_1 \left( \overline{x}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \right) - \hat{\beta}_0 \left( \overline{x}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \right) \\ =& \overline{x}_0\left( \hat{\beta}_1 - \hat{\beta}_0 \right) + \hat{\beta}_0 \left( \overline{x}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \right) + \left( \hat{\beta}_1 - \hat{\beta}_0 \right) \left( \overline{x}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \right) \end{split} \]

  • “3” for threefold decomposition

Interaction term discussion

  • It still includes:
    • unexplained
    • explained
  • It also includes interaction between coefficients and characteristics
  • Hmmm, what does that measure/represent?
  • Cross-group differences in explanatory variables and coefficients can occur at the same time

Some Decomp Issues

What is the right base group?

  • Our previous version…

\[ \begin{split} \hat\Delta_2 =& \overline{x}_1 \hat{\beta}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_0 + \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_1 \\ =& \overline{x}_0\left( \hat{\beta}_1 - \hat{\beta}_0 \right) + \hat{\beta}_1 \left( \overline{x}_1 - \overline{x}_0 \right) \end{split} \]

  • What if we reversed the difference?

\[ \begin{split} \hat\Delta_2 =& \overline{x}_0 \hat{\beta}_0 - \overline{x}_1 \hat{\beta}_1 + \overline{x}_1 \hat{\beta}_0 - \overline{x}_1 \hat{\beta}_0 \\ =& \overline{x}_1\left( \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 \right) + \hat{\beta}_0 \left( \overline{x}_0 - \overline{x}_1 \right) \end{split} \]

Questions

  • Are these equal?
  • When might they be?
  • Researchers have suggested many “averages” to address this

What happens with dummies/categories in a decomposition?

  • Look at a simplistic regression
  • Here, \(d\) has three levels, one of which is left out
  • For completeness, \(d_j = \mathbf{1}(D=j)\), \(j=\{1,2,3\}\)

\[ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 d_{i1} + \beta_2 d_{2i} + e_i \]

  • As with the choice of base for the decomposition regression
  • The base of the categories affects the “math”

Add back the left-out dummy?

  • Consider this version of the model

\[ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 d_{i1} + \beta_2 d_{2i} + \beta_3 d_{3i} + e_i \]

  • That would give a DV trap
  • But, can “restrict” \(\beta_3 = 0\)
  • Define: \(a = \sum_{j=1}^3 \beta_j /J\), here, \(J=3\)

Relation between \(a\) and the \(\beta\)s?

  • With \(a\) above and \((j \neq 0)\)
    • \(\tilde\beta_0 = \beta_0 + a\)
    • \(\tilde\beta_j = \beta_j - a\)
  • The above model is equivalent to:

\[ y_i = \tilde\beta_0 + \tilde\beta_1 d_{i1} + \tilde\beta_2 d_{2i} + \tilde\beta_3 d_{3i} + e_i \]

  • Subject to the restriction: \(\sum_j \tilde{\beta} = 0\)

Brief Aside on Contrasts

Dummy coding in R

  • “Dummy” is how economists normally use categories
  • For example, factor.var <- factor(var, levels = , labels =)
    • Levels can be used to create a baseline: first is the base
    • Labels match the order of the levels

Releveling

  • So, could redefine the factor, relevel the factor or
  • Can also use contr.treatment() to change the baseline
    • For example factor.var <- contr.treatment(3, base = 2)
    • This uses group 2 as the baseline of the three levels

Standard dummy contrasts

  • Under our ‘dummy’ approach, the intercept is the left-out mean
  • This is a type of “contrast”; for contr.treatment(3)
contr.treatment(3)
#>   2 3
#> 1 0 0
#> 2 1 0
#> 3 0 1
  • Group 1 is base, and represents intercept
  • Column 2 compares variable 2 to base
  • Column 3 compares variable 3 to base

Sum Effects coding in R

  • One could consider a “sum”
  • It compares mean of a dependent variable for a given level to the overall mean of the dependent variable contr.sum(3)
contr.sum(3)
#>   [,1] [,2]
#> 1    1    0
#> 2    0    1
#> 3   -1   -1

Interpret

  • First column compares variable 1 to the grand mean
  • Second compares variable 2 to the grand mean
  • Grand mean here is the mean of the means of the dependent variable across levels

Helmert effects in R

  • Helmert coding compares each level of a categorical variable to the mean of subsequent levels of the variable
  • myhelmert2 <- matrix(c(2/3, -1/3, -1/3, 0, .5, -.5), ncol = 2)
  • myhelmert2

    • Decomp Group: Households with 1 child v Households with 0

The “Question”

  • Is there a difference in minimum required income, due to having a child in the household?

  • Is that difference related to differences in total household income (explained) or differences in the relationship between income and minimum income across groups (unexplained)?
